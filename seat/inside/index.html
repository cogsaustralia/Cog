Seat at the Table

What one share actually gives you and how to use it

A free guide from Thomas Cunliffe · Drake Village NSW · Wahlubal Country, Bundjalung Nation

You do not need millions to influence a mining company.
You need one share.

This guide backs up the CEO Power Cheat Sheet. The Cheat Sheet told you that one share gives you real power over a mining company. This guide explains exactly why that is true. Based on real laws in Australia right now.

Four things to know. Five minutes to read.

1. You get a vote at the AGM

Every company listed on the ASX must hold an Annual General Meeting. That is the law. Corporations Act 2001, section 250N.

At that meeting, shareholders vote on real decisions. Who sits on the board. How executives get paid. Whether big transactions go ahead.

One share gets you into the room. One share gives you a vote.

Why does that matter?

Because the CEO has to sit in that room and listen to every registered shareholder. Not protesters outside the gate. Not a petition. Shareholders inside the meeting, on the record.

Under the Corporations Act, any shareholder can:

  • Attend the AGM in person or by proxy
  • Ask questions about the company activities
  • Vote on resolutions put to the meeting
  • Submit resolutions for other shareholders to vote on

When five thousand people all hold shares in the same company, that is five thousand votes. Five thousand voices on the register. The board cannot pretend that community does not exist.

2. ESG laws put money on your voice

EY Global Mining and Metals survey ranks loss of social licence to operate as the number one business risk in the sector. Social licence means community support. If a community is against a project, that is a financial risk.

The ASX Corporate Governance Principles require listed companies to identify and manage material risks. Community opposition to a mining project is now a material risk. That means it has to be reported to investors.

What does that mean for you?

It means your voice has a dollar value. When a community is organised and holds shares, boards have to take it seriously. Not because they want to. Because their investors are watching.

Community opposition is not noise anymore.
It is a financial risk boards must report on.

Companies that ignore organised shareholders face questions from institutional investors, superannuation funds, and asset managers who have their own ESG obligations. The pressure runs all the way up.

3. The board has a legal duty to respond to you

When a registered shareholder writes to a company about a material concern, the board has obligations under the Corporations Act and the ASX Listing Rules.

You are not a member of the public sending a letter. You are a shareholder. That is a different legal category. The company has duties to you.

The words that open the door

As a registered shareholder I am writing to raise a concern about social licence and ESG compliance in relation to [project name].

Use this sentence. It puts your letter in a different category. The company legal and governance team must treat it as a formal shareholder communication.

That one sentence changes everything. You are no longer someone asking for a favour. You are a stakeholder with legal standing, raising a compliance concern.
