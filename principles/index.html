Foundation doctrine

The framing principles

Three ideas that explain what kind of project this is — and what kind it is not.

Principle one

Civic Synergy

"The multiplier effect of small commitments combining into structural capability."

$4  once only × 1,000,000 Australians = structural voice

No individual Australian can buy a meaningful stake in Santos or Origin Energy. The cost of a single lot of shares is manageable; the cost of a seat at the table — enough shares to attend an AGM, file a resolution, nominate a director — is beyond any household. One person cannot do it.

A million Australians paying $4 once can. That is civic synergy: the multiplication of small, individually inconsequential commitments into something that no corporation, government, or lobby group can replicate. Not because of ideology, but because of arithmetic.

Principle two

Community Cohesion

"The durable trust between members, between the Trustee and members, and between the Foundation and our First Nations governance partners."

A member of the COG$ Foundation is not an investor. An investor puts money in expecting a return. A member makes a once-only contribution to a community joint venture and receives a voice — in governance decisions, in AGM attendance, in the direction of the Foundation's stewardship work.

That distinction matters legally, structurally, and relationally. The Foundation holds ASX-listed shares on behalf of members under a deed of trust. The trust relationship requires ongoing good faith — from the Caretaker Trustee to members, from members to each other, and from the Foundation to the First Nations communities whose country these resources come from. Community cohesion is what makes that trust durable across decades, not just during the excitement of launch.

Principle three

Australian Community Fellowship

"The recognition that Australians, taken together as a community of place, country, and people, possess agency that is structurally distinct from voters, taxpayers, consumers, or constituents of any party."

Every five years, Australians vote. Every year, Australians pay tax. Every day, Australians consume. None of those acts, individually or together, gives Australians a direct say in whether the companies extracting minerals from their country pay a fair share, engage fairly with First Nations communities, or plan responsibly for a changing energy system.

The Foundation operates in a different register. It is not a political campaign. It is not a consumer boycott. It is not a petition. It is a community joint venture constituted under a deed of trust — which means its authority derives from the common law of contract and equity, not from electoral outcomes or parliamentary goodwill. No election can revoke it. No advertising budget can outbid it on the CHESS register. That is pre-political stewardship: Australian Community Fellowship.

These three principles together describe what makes COG$ a different kind of project. Not a political party. Not a campaign. Not a fund. A community joint venture under a deed of trust, funded by Australians, owing nothing to any corporation, party, or commentator.
