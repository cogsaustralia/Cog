## Foundation Statement — Final, Locked 4 May 2026

For as long as human civilisation has organised itself around land and resources, the people closest to the ground have had the least formal say in what happens to the wealth beneath it. That is not a modern failure. It is the oldest power structure on earth, and every political system built on top of it has inherited and reproduced it.

COGS is the bridge.

It starts with the legal tools that exist right now but are not formally used by the communities they were always meant to serve. Shares. AGM votes. The legal right of a registered shareholder to stand in the room, ask the CEO a question, and get an answer on the record. These are not revolutionary tools. They are the existing tools of the existing system, picked up and handed to the communities the existing system was never designed to include.

That is how the bridge is built. Not by tearing down what exists. By crossing it.

COGS — the Community Owned Governance System — is a permanent, legally entrenched, community joint venture. It pools real ownership in real assets and uses that ownership to give communities direct, enforceable governance rights. One person, one vote. Not one dollar, one vote. It is not a political party, a protest movement, or a campaign. It is built to outlast any government, any party, any CEO, and any trend.

Its deepest principle: the wealth beneath the ground does not have to be extracted to be real. The minerals beneath this Country are already measurable, already appreciating, already priced into equity markets every day. The communities above them — most profoundly the First Nations peoples whose custodianship of Country predates every modern institution — deserve an enforceable seat at the governance table before extraction becomes the default. Not after. Not as a consultation. As a legal right, built into the structure from day one.

The first cog started turning over a beer at the Lunatic Hotel in Drake, on Wahlubal Country, Bundjalung Nation. A few hundred dollars. One seat at one table.

Where it goes from here, and where it ends up, is up to the people who know the system is fundamentally broken and that it is time to change.

The structure is the bridge. The community is the crossing. The Country is what we are crossing toward.

## Eight Governing Principles — Final, Locked 4 May 2026

**Principle 1 — One Person, One Vote.**
Every member of COGS has exactly the same say as every other member. It does not matter if you joined with $4 or $4 million. Your vote counts the same. Money does not buy more voice here. This is the rule that makes COGS different from every other system most of us have lived under.

**Principle 2 — The Ground Is Already Wealth.**
The gold, gas, copper, and minerals beneath this Country are worth real money right now, today, whether anyone digs them up or not. Mining and resource companies put a dollar value on those minerals every single day and trade that value on the stock market. The communities living above those minerals — and the First Nations peoples whose Country it is — should have a say in what happens to that wealth before anyone decides to dig. Not after. The decision to dig, to wait, or to protect is a community decision, not just a company decision.

**Principle 3 — First Nations Partnership Means Real Power, Not a Tick in a Box.**
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have cared for this Country for tens of thousands of years. That relationship with Country is not history. It is authority. Inside COGS, First Nations communities do not get consulted at the end of the process. They hold real governance rights — enforceable legal rights built into the structure from day one — that cannot be voted away or removed by anyone. If a decision affects Country and First Nations peoples say no, that no counts.

**Principle 4 — We Use the System's Own Tools.**
COGS does not fight the system from outside the fence. It walks through the front door. A share in a company listed on the Australian Stock Exchange (ASX) gives the person who holds it the legal right to attend the company's annual meeting (called the AGM), ask the CEO questions, and vote on real decisions. These rights already exist in Australian law. Most ordinary Australians have never used them because they have never owned the shares. COGS changes that. We pick up the tools the system built, and we hand them to the communities the system forgot.

**Principle 5 — This Is Forever, Not a Phase.**
COGS is not a campaign that ends after an election. It is not a fund that closes when the market moves. It is a permanent structure, held under South Australian trust law, which means it can run without an end date. No single person — not the founder, not a trustee, not a big donor — can shut it down or take the assets for themselves. The structure holds. The community's ownership compounds over time, year after year, for as long as people choose to stand together in it.

**Principle 6 — The Strength Is in the Numbers.**
One person standing alone outside a mining company's gate has no legal power over what that company does. One thousand people who each own a share in that company, and who vote together at the AGM, change the conversation in the room entirely. Four people with a million dollars have no power compared to a million people with four. The more people who join COGS, the stronger the community's voice becomes. No individual — including the person who built this — decides where it goes. The members decide together.

**Principle 7 — Everything Is on the Record.**
Every important decision made by COGS is written down, time-stamped, and stored in a way that cannot be changed after the fact. Members can check their own record at any time. COGS does not ask anyone to trust a person. It asks people to trust a documented process that anyone can read and verify. If something goes wrong, the record shows what happened and who decided it.

**Principle 8 — Community First. Always.**
The money side of COGS — the dividends (that is, the share of profits paid out to members), the services, the trade network — exists to make communities stronger. It is the engine, not the destination. The moment the money starts driving the community instead of the other way around, the project has lost its way. At least thirty cents in every dollar that goes into the charitable part of COGS must go directly to First Nations programs. That is not a goal. It is locked in. It cannot be changed without the agreement of the community.
